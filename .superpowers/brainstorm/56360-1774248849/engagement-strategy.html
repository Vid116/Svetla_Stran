How do we hook a first-time visitor?

Someone just finished reading an article they found via Google/social. They have 30 seconds before they close the tab. What keeps them?

The Problem

Right now, after reading an article, they see: share bar → related articles → newsletter signup → comments. This is what every blog does. Nothing says "this site is different, come back."

Meanwhile, your database has rich emotional data (antidote_for, emotions, scores) that readers never see. Your unique "antidote" philosophy is invisible to visitors.

Three strategic approaches — click to select

These aren't mutually exclusive, but which resonates most as the primary direction?

A

🧭 Emotional Navigation

Core idea: Let readers browse by how they FEEL, not by category.

"Feeling cynical? Here's your antidote." / "I need warmth" / "Show me something that restores hope"

Surface the antidote_for and emotions data as a navigation layer. On each article, show what emotional poison this story counteracts. After reading, suggest the next story based on emotional continuity.

Why it hooks: No other news site does this. It turns passive reading into active emotional self-care. The reader thinks "this site understands what I need" instead of "this is another article list."

Strengths

  • Completely unique differentiator
  • Uses data you already collect
  • Creates emotional connection with the brand
  • Makes the "antidote" philosophy tangible

Risks

  • Might feel gimmicky if not done tastefully
  • Emotion labels need careful Slovenian wording
  • Some visitors might not connect with the concept
B

💛 Emotional Reactions + Social Proof

Core idea: Replace generic "likes" with emotion-specific reactions that match your 5 feelings.

After reading: "How did this story make you feel?" → PONOS / TOPLINA / OLAJŠANJE / ČUDENJE / UPANJE (with friendly icons). Show counts: "47 readers felt warmth from this story."

Add a "trending stories" section based on reaction velocity. Show "Most warming story this week" type highlights.

Why it hooks: Creates community feeling — "other people are here, feeling what I feel." Readers become participants, not just consumers. Social proof drives clicks on other articles.

Strengths

  • Low friction engagement (one click)
  • Creates social proof and community feel
  • Generates data for better recommendations
  • Familiar pattern (like Facebook reactions) but unique twist

Risks

  • Need enough traffic for counts to feel real
  • Could feel empty/lonely if counts are low early on
  • Reaction fatigue (another thing to click)
C

🎯 Redesigned End-of-Article Experience

Core idea: The moment someone finishes reading is your ONE chance. Redesign the post-article flow to be a guided journey, not a list of unrelated CTAs.

After finishing: a "What to read next" card that's emotionally connected ("If this story moved you, this one will too"), a "Daily Dose" signup (not generic newsletter — "get one story a day that's an antidote for doom-scrolling"), and a "Story of the Day" highlight that creates daily freshness.

The key insight: instead of scattering CTAs (newsletter here, share there, comments below), create ONE cohesive post-read experience that feels like a natural continuation.

Strengths

  • Directly targets the B) conversion moment
  • Improves existing components, less new code
  • "Daily Dose" reframe is more compelling than "newsletter"
  • Emotionally-connected recommendations beat category-based

Risks

  • Less revolutionary, more evolutionary
  • Success depends on recommendation quality
  • Doesn't create new engagement loops, just optimizes existing

💡 My recommendation

I'd go A + C combined. The emotional navigation (A) gives you the unique identity that makes someone think "I've never seen anything like this." The redesigned end-of-article (C) is where that identity converts into retention. B (reactions) is great but works best AFTER you have traffic — empty reaction counts hurt more than help early on.

The combination: reader finishes an article → sees "This story was an antidote for cynicism" → below that, "Need more? Browse by what you need:" with emotion-based links → then a "Daily Dose" signup instead of generic newsletter → then emotionally-matched related articles instead of same-category ones.
